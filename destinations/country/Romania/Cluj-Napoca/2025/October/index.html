Cluj-Napoca in October: Youthful Energy & Autumn Colors

Visited in October 2025 • Best season: Autumn

Cluj-Napoca is Romania’s cultural and academic heart — a lively, youthful city full of cafes, parks, art, and a vibrant atmosphere. October adds golden colors to the historic center, making it perfect for exploring on foot.

What I Experienced

Cluj-Napoca feels alive from the moment you arrive. Students everywhere, art on every corner, historic squares filled with outdoor terraces, and the calm, wide parks that cut through the city.

Unirii Square is the heart of everything — with its huge cathedral, cafés, and the Matthias Corvinus statue standing proudly in the center. In October, the light is warm, the air is crisp, and the whole city feels photogenic.

Even though Cluj is modern and energetic, it’s also incredibly walkable, safe, and charming — a perfect blend of youth and history.

What to See & Do in Cluj-Napoca

Unirii Square

Union Square (Piața Unirii) is the largest and most important square in Cluj-Napoca, serving as the central point from which the historic district extends.
It features major landmarks such as St. Michael’s Church—the tallest church tower in Romania—along with the Matthias Corvinus statue, the old town hall, the National Museum of Art, and several historic palaces. Historically known as “Big Market Square,”
it was distinguished from the smaller “Little Market Square,” now called Museum Square.

Lucian Blaga National Theatre

The theatre building, located in Cluj-Napoca in Romania, was constructed between 1904 and 1906 in a neo-Baroque style and designed by Austrian architects Ferdinand Fellner and Hermann Helmer. It opened on 8 September 1906, and has a seating capacity of 928.
Initially serving the Hungarian National Theatre until 1919, after which it became home to the Romanian National Theatre and Romanian Opera under Romanian administration.
The building is listed in Romania’s National Register of Historic Monuments and continues to host both classical and modern dramatic productions.

Catedrala Mitropolitană „Adormirea Maicii Domnului”

The Orthodox Cathedral in Cluj-Napoca, built between 1920 and 1930 after the union of Transylvania with Romania, is one of the city’s most important religious landmarks and is dedicated to the Dormition of the Mother of God. It became an archiepiscopal cathedral in 1973 and, since 2006, has served as the cathedral of the Archdiocese of Vad, Feleac, and Cluj, as well as the seat of the Cluj, Maramureș, and Sălaj Metropolis. Today, it is Romania’s third-tallest cathedral and is officially listed as a historic monument.

Old town

n the heart of Cluj-Napoca’s Old Town, you’ll find the captivating Art Museum of Cluj‑Napoca, housed in the historic Bánffy Palace and filled with Romanian and European art dating from the 15th to 20th centuries.
After your museum visit, step into one of the excellent local eateries like Roata, which specialises in traditional Romanian cuisine—think sarmale (stuffed cabbage rolls) and papanăşi (Romanian doughnuts) in a cosy, atmospheric setting.
Whether you’re lingering over a good meal or wandering cobbled streets, the Old Town makes it easy to combine culture and cuisine in one wander-worthy spot.

On the way to the viewpoint, I came across the cafe 'Nish', which was closed because it was Sunday. And we came to the hill where 'Cetățuia Park' is located. From there, the view extends over the entire city of Cluj-Napoca.
